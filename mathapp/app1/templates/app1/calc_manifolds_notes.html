Our introductory calculus courses provided us with tools to compute derivatives and integrals that were seemingly independent of the space that was chosen as a background for such methods. For example, to compute the velocity of a model rocket (the rate of change of its position), we don't care if the measurement was carried out in New York, Tokyo or Paris: we would expect the same answer in all cases. Calculus on Manifolds takes into account the space in which we are making use of our calculus tools. A manifold is that space which we are referring to and can be of any arbitrary dimension. Take the example of a GPS satellite that is orbitting the Earth: the manifold is one that is four dimensional and is often referred to as spacetime.
