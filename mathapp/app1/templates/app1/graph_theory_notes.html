History of Graph Theory

Consider the 18th century problem of the seven bridges of Konigsberg. In one of the areas of the city lie a network of rivers and bridges, as seen in the diagram below.

The problem is outlined as follows: Is there a way for a person to travel along each bridge, but only exactly once? In the year 1736, Euler was able to prove that the answer is a definitive no. Given a few trial runs, we may be able to convince ourselves that Euler's conclusion is indeed correct. Only by developing a new form of mathematical analysis, graph theory, was Euler able to show with absolute certainty that indeed there is no solution to this problem, as well as solving a whole host of related problems.

In the map above, we can consider the city of Konisberg to be a planar space. We can think of each land mass as a vertex and the bridges connecting the land masses as edges connecting the vertices:

In this way, we are only keeping information relevant to the problem in the diagram and removing all the other details. These schematics like the one above are known as graphs.

Terminology of Graphs

Vertices are points on a graph that represent some kind of data. These vertices can be connected by edges. Faces are parts of the plane that are enclosed by edges. If we can draw these vertices and edges such that no edges intersect each other, the graph is called a planar graph.



In the example shown above, the vertices are A, B, C and D. The edges are AB, AC, BC and CD as they connect these vertices. There are two faces: the one enclosed by ABC, and the rest of the plane. The graph above is planar because the edges do not intersect eachother.

The degree of a vertex is equivalent to the number of edges that extrude from it. In the example above, the degree of vertex C is 3, while the degree of vertex D is 1. The order of the graph is just the number of vertices it contains. The above example has order 4. Two vertices are said to be adjacent if there is an edge that connects them. Graphs and their Degree Sequences

We begin this section with the following theorem. Let G = (V,E) be a finite simple graph. Then

\sum_{v \epsilon V} deg(v) = 2|E|

This is known as the degree sum theorem. In words: the sum of the degrees of all vertices is twice the number of edges. The proof for this is rather trivial: every edge must be connected to two and only two vertices. QED

A degree sequence of G = (V,E) is the sequence of degrees of vertices of G in decreasing order. A sequence of non-negative integers in decreasing order is graphic if it is the degree sequence of some graph. Let's look at some examples.

Ex. The degree sequence given by 1, 1, 1 is not graphic because it cannot represent a graph. In this case, the graph would have to contain three vertices, and each vertice would have to be adjacent to exactly one other vertice. This is impossible.

Ex. The degree sequence given by 1, 1, 1, 1 is graphic. See the diagram below for an example of a graph with this degree sequence.

We now come to the hand shaking lemma. Let G = (V,E) be a finite simple graph. Then the number of vertices in G with odd degree must be an even amount. Stated otherwise:

V = (V_{even}) U (V_{odd}) \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} V_{odd} \ mod(2) = 0

So, for example, we know that the degree sequence given by 5, 4, 3, 2, 2, 2, 1 is not graphic. A degree sequence like 4, 3, 2, 2, 2, 1, 1, 1, 0 could be graphic, but we do not know from the hand shaking lemma. We simply cannot rule out the possibility that it is graphic from the lemma. A better tool for this is theorem by Hakimi, which we now state.

Consider sequences given by

(1) s_{1}, t_{1}, ... , t_{s}, d_{1},...,d_{n} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} (2) t_{1}-1, ... , t_{s}-1, d_{1},...,d_{n}

Where both sequences are decreasing. Then (1) is graphic if and only if (2) is graphic. Take the following sequence: 6, 6, 6, 6, 4, 3, 3, 0. We begin by observing that we cannot dismiss this sequence as not graphical from the hand shakeing lemma. However, we can see that the sequence takes the form of (1) in the above theorem. Applying the theorem, we get

6, 6, 6, 6, 4, 3, 3, 0 \\ \phantom{aaa} \ \ \ 5, 5, 5, 3, 2, 2, 0 \\ \phantom{aaa} \ \ \ \ \ \4, 4, 2, 1, 1, 0 \\ \phantom{aaa} \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ 3, 1, 0, 0, 0 \\

The last sequence is clearly not graphic, as show below:

So then we know the original sequence cannot be graphic either.

In considering graphs, we are especially interested in the structure of each graph (ex its degree, its degree sequence, etc.) rather than how it can be drawn. Take the following two graphs:

Though these two graphs have the same number of degree and edges, they differ in degree sequence. The first graph has a degree sequence of 3, 1, 1, 1 while the second graph has a degree sequence of 2, 2, 1, 1. These two graphs are structurally different. This leads us to the following definition.

Two finite simple graphs

G_{1} = (V_{1},E_{1}) \ \ \ G_{2} = (V_{2},E_{2})

are said to be isomorphic if there exist a bijection

f: \ V_{1} \ \longrightarrow \ V_{2}

that satisfies the following condition: For and u and v in V1 that are adjacent in G1, f(u) and f(v) are in V2 and are adjacent in G2.

The diagram below shows all graphs of degree 3 up to isomorphism.

There are certain structural properties of a graph that will remain unchanged under isomorphism. Such a concept is known as isomorphic invariance of a graph. There are many examples of these, and we will discuss a few now.

The order of a graph G = (V,E) : |V| is one such isomorphic invariant. In other words, if we have to graphs that are isomorphic, then they must contain the same number of vertices. This is a trivial result.

The degree sequence of a graph must be isomorphic invariant, as must the sum of all degrees of a graphs vertices. From this it also follows that the number of edges of a graph is an isomorphic invariant.



Graph Classes

Certain types of graphs are important and frequent enough that they deserve their own classification. Here is a selection of some of these graphs.

Path of length n

P_{n} = (V = \{0,1,...,n\}, \ E = \{(i,i+1) \ | \ i = 0,...n-1 \})

Cycle of length n

C_{n} = (V = \mathbb{Z} / \mathbb{Z}_{n}, \ E = \{(i,i+1) \ | \ i \ \epsilon \ \mathbb{Z} / \mathbb{Z}_{n} \} )

Complete graph on n vertices

K_{n} = (V = \{(1,...,n\}, \ E = [V]^{2} )

In other words, all vertices are connected to each other.

Complete bi-partite graph on n and m vertices

K_{n,m} = (V = V_{1} \ U \ V_{2}, \ E = \{(v_{1},v_{2}) \ | \ v_{1} \ \epsilon \ V_{1} \ \ v_{2} \ \epsilon \ V_{2} ) \\ \phantom{aaa} |V_{1}| = n, \ |V_{2}| = m

In other words, every vertex in V1 connects with a vertex in V2, however no vertices in V1 are adjacent with other vertices in V1, and the same is true of V2.

Wheel on n-vertices

W_{n} = C_{n} + V(\text{connected to every vertex in C})

Adjacency Matrices and Graph Eigenvalues

Let's look at the following two isomorphic graphs:

We can create an adjancency matrix for each graph, where the ij-th entry is equivalent to 1 if the vertex i is adjacenty to the vertex j, and 0 otherwise. In the first example, the matrix would be

\begin{bmatrix} 0&1&0 \\ 1&0&1 \\ 0&1&0 \end{bmatrix}

And the second matrix would be given by:

\begin{bmatrix} 0&1&1 \\ 1&0&0 \\ 1&0&0 \end{bmatrix}

There are some properties of adjacency matrices that are evident:

  • All diagonal entries must be 0
  • The sum of entries in the ith row or column is the same as the degree as the ith vertex
  • The sum of all entries in all rows/columns is the same as the sum of the degrees of all vertices of the graph
  • They are symmetric
  • The sum of all eigenvalues are zero

This last point is not as obvious. But in fact it implies something stronger: all adjaceny matrices of a graph G = (V,E) have the same eigenvalues, regardless of how we choose to enumerate the vertcies. In other words: the characteristic polynomial of a graph G is the characteristic polynomial for any of the adjacency matrices of G.

An induced subgraph of a graph G = (V,E) is a subgraph H = (V',E'), where V' is a subset of V, and E' contains all the edges in G between elements in V'.

Given a finite simple graph G, we define the following quantities:

\Delta(G) = \max_{v \ \epsilon \ V} \{deg(V)\} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} \delta(G) = \min_{v \ \epsilon \ V} \{deg(V)\}

Now we have the following theorem which relates the spectrum of eigenvalues of a graph G to the maximum of the above values. Suppose G is a finite simple graph. Then

-\Delta(G) \ \leq \ \lambda \ \leq \ \Delta(G) \ \ \forall \ \lambda \ \epsilon \ spec(G)

Proof: Let x be an eigenvector that is not 0. Then we have

Ax = \lambda x \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} |\lambda||x_{i}| = |\lambda x_{i}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = |(\lambda x)_{i}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = |(Ax)_{i}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = |\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij}x_{j}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} \leq \sum_{j=1}^{n} (a_{ij}|x_{j}|) \ \ \(by \ \Delta \ inequality) \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} \leq (\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij})|x_{i}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = deg(V_{i}|x_{i} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} \implies |\lambda||x_{i}| \ \leq \ \Delta(G)|x_{i}| \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} |\lambda| \ \leq \ \Delta(G)

QED

(i) If G is a tree, then V = |E| + 1.

A forest is any acyclic graph. A graph G = (V,E) is a forest if |V| = |E| + N, where N is the number of connected components.

If e is a bridge in G and H1 and H2 are banks, then we have: s(G) = s(H1)s(H2), where s stands for the number of spanning trees in a graph. In the above example, s(H1) = 3 and s(H2) = 3, so s(G) = 9.

Recall that Kn is the complete graph on n vertices. Then we have the following fact about the number of spanning trees for Kn:

s(k_{n}) = n^{n-2}

Take the example of k3 below. We can find 3 trees for k3, and indeed, 3 to the power of 3-2 is 3.

Note also that although each tree is isomorphic to the other, this is not what we are interested in. We are focused on how many trees we can make that are not identitical up to a labelling of the vertices.

Recall that Kn,m is the complete bipartite graph on m and n vertices. Then we have the following fact about the number of spanning trees for Kn,m:

s(k_{n,m}) = m^{n-1}n^{m-1}

In general, it is difficult to simply enumerate by the naked eye how many spanning trees a particular graph G may have. Fortunately, there is the following theorem that will help us compute this quantity for a graph G = (V,E).

Let G = (V,E) be a graph with adjacency matrix A. If D is the matrix given by

D = \begin{bmatrix} d_{1}&0&...&0 \\ 0&d_{2}&...&0 \\ 0&0&...&d_{n} \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} d_{i} = deg(V_{i})

Then we define the matrix L = D - A and the matrix M obtained by deleting the last row and the last column of L. Then we have s(G) = det(M).

Consider the following example:

For this graph, we have:

D = \begin{bmatrix} 2&0&0&0 \\ 0&2&0&0 \\ 0&0&1&0 \\ 0&0&0&3 \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} A = \begin{bmatrix} 0&1&0&1 \\ 1&0&0&1 \\ 0&0&0&1 \\ 1&1&1&0 \\ \end{bmatrix}

We obtain L from the difference of D and A, and M from the deletion of the 4th row and 4th column of L:

L = \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0&1 \\ -1&2&0&-1 \\ 0&0&1&0 \\ -1&-1&-1&3 \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} M = \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0 \\ -1&2&0 \\ 0&0&1 \\ \end{bmatrix}

From this we have det(M) = 3. In this case, we can directly count the number of spanning trees of G by construcing them if we please.



Vertex Colorings, Critical Graphs

So far we have only discussed graphs without putting any emphasis on vertex labelling. There are a few ways to include labelling, and one works as follows. Suppose we have some graph G = (V,E) and we want to label the vertices on the graph such that no two adjacent vertices share the same label. This type of labelling is known as vertex coloring. The term coloring comes from the idea that we might use colors to label our vertices. This, however, introduces a quick inconvenience as we make our graphs arbitrarily large. Instead, we will "color" our graphs using numbers and say that a graph G has a vertex coloring if no two adjacent vertices share the same number.

Having this definition, a natural question now arises. Given a graph G = (V,E), what is the minimal amount of labels we can use so that the graph is vertex colored? This leads us to the following definition:

Chromatic number of graph G = (V,E):

\mathcal{X}(G) = inf\{n \ | \ \exists \ \text{a n-coloring of G}\}

So the Chromatic number, in words, is the smallest possible number of labels (n) that we can use to create the a vertex coloring of G. Take the following examples:

The graph on the left is not a vertex coloring since there are two vertices adjacent to each other both with the label of one. The middle graph is a vertex coloring since no two adjacent vertices share the same label. The graph on the right is also a vertex coloring, and in addition also contains the minimum number of labels to make this possible. Therefore, the chromatic number of C5 is 3.

Here are some useful facts about particular graph classes and their chromatic number:

\mathcal{X}(C_{n}) = \begin{cases} 2,& \text{if n is even } \\ 3, & \text{if n is odd} \\ \end{cases}
\mathcal{X}(K_{n}) = n \\ \\
\mathcal{X}(W_{n}) = \begin{cases} 3,& \text{if n is even } \\ 4, & \text{if n is odd} \\ \end{cases}

Another natural question that arises is in relation to subgraphs of a vertex colored graph G. That is, if we remove a vertex from G, is it possible that the new subgraph, say H, has the same chromatic number? In general this is possible, but in special cases, there is no vertex we could remove from G that would not reduce the chromatic number of the new subgraph. This leads us to the following definition.

A graph G = (V,E) is called critical if for any proper subgraph H of G, the chromatic number of H is strictly less that the chromatic number of G.

To see an example of this, consider the graph of the wheel on 5 vertices, W5.

The graph on the left is W5, which has chromatic number four. Now if we want to show that it is critical, we need to show that any proper subgraph of W5 has a chromatic number less than four. At first this may seem daunting, but we can realize the following: there are only two subgraphs, say H1 and H2, of W5 up to isomorphism that are created by the removal of one edge. Therefore, if we can show these two subgraphs of W5 have a chromatic number less than four, we are done. We don't have to test any other proper subgraphs of W5 since they will be considered proper subgraphs of the graphs H1 and H2 and therefore have a chromatic number less than or equal to the chromatic numbers of H1 and H2. As you can see in the center and right graphs, the chromatic numbers of these two proper subgraphs are both three. Thus we have shown that W5 is a critical graph.

We now state the following two facts about critical graphs:

  1. If a graph G = (V,E) is critical, it must be connected
  2. For a graph G = (V,E), there exists a subgraph H with H being critical and the chromatic number of H is equivalent to that of G
  3. If a graph G = (V,E) is critical, then the degree of any vertex v in V must be greater than or equal to the chromatic number of G minus 1:
    4. deg(v) \ \geq \ \mathcal{X}(G) - 1
  4. If a graph G = (V,E) is critical, then
    5. (\mathcal{X}(G) - 1) \ \leq \ 2|E|

A couple of more definitions: The girth of a graph G is the length of the shortest cycle in G. If no cycle exists in G, the girth is said to be infinite.

The distance between two points x and y in G, denoted d(x,y), is the shortest number of edges between x and y

Something that we won't go into detail on, but mention here because of its importance in graph theory is the following: suppose we have a graph G = (V,E), and suppose that C(n) is the number of ways to n-color G. Then it is a fact that there exists a polynomial, say P(x), with the following properties:

  • P(n) = C(n)
  • the degree of P is equal to the degree of G
  • P(x) has a leading coefficient of 1, and in fact, all its coefficients are integers

P(x) is called the chromatic polynomial of G.



Edge Colorings

So far, we have defined a way to color graphs using its vertices. Another logical way to color a graph would be using its edges. Let G = (V,E) be a graph. A proper edge k-coloring of G is one where we can find a labelling of E such that no two edges share the same label. (NB - an edge coloring is simply ANY labelling of E. A proper edge k-coloring is a much stronger condition)

The edge chromatic number of G is the smallest number of labels needed for a proper k-coloring of G. This number is denoted X'(G). Now we arrive at the follwing facts:

(i) \ \mathcal{X}'(G) \ \geq \ \Delta G = \max_{v \ \epsilon \ V} \{deg(v)\} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} (ii) \ \text{(Vizing Theorem) If G = (V,E) is regular, then} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} \Delta G \ \leq \ \mathcal{X}'(G) \ \leq \ \Delta G + 1 \\ \phantom{aaa} \text{recall a regular graph is one where each vertex has the same degree} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} (iii) \ \mathcal{X}'(K_{2n}) = 2n - 1 = \mathcal{X}'(K_{2n-1})
