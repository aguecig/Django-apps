Question 1: A farmer has 750 metres of fencing that he will use to make an enclosure for his sheep. He will make a rectangular enclosure that has a river as one side of its border, so that his sheep might have somewhere to freely drink. What dimensions should the farmer use for his enclosure in order to maximize the space created for his sheep?

We let L represent the length and W represent the width of the enclosure and A represent its area. Set up two equations: a constraint for the perimeter of the fencing and the area of the enclosure which is to be optimized.

750 = L + 2W \\ \phantom{aaa} A = LW

Notice how the perimeter has only one length accounted for. This is because one of the sides is bordered by the river, so no fencing is required. Now we isolate for L in the constraint equation and substitute that expression into the area equation.

L = 750 - 2W \\ \phantom{aaa} A = (750 - 2W)(W) \\ \phantom{aaa} A = 750W - 2W^{2}

To optimize the area, we take the derivative with respect to W, set A' to zero and solve for W.

A' = 750 - 4W \\ \phantom{aaa} 0 = 750 - 4W \\ \phantom{aaa} W = 187.5

Finally, use this value of W to find the value of L that will maximize the area of the enclosure.

L = 750 - 2W \\ \phantom{aaa} L = 750 - 2(187.5) \\ \phantom{aaa} L = 375

The dimensions of the pen that will maximize the area of the enclosure are 375m x 187.5m. Notice that 375 + 187.5 + 187.5 = 750, exactly the amount of fencing we had to use.

Question 2: A storage container is to be constructed with a rectangular base and an open top. The width of the container needs to be set at 4 metres, while the volume must be exactly 36 cubic metres. The material used for the base of the container is $10 per square metre and the material used for the sides is $5 per square metre. What dimensions for the length and height should be used in order to minimize the cost of the storage container?

We will let L, and H represent the length and height of the container, respectively and let C reprsent the cost of the container. Now we make two equations. The first is the volume, which is our constraint. The second is the cost, which is to be minimized.

V = WLH \\ \phantom{aaa} 36 = 4LH \\ \phantom{aaa} 9 = LH \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} C = 5(2WH) + 5(2LH) + 10(WL) \\ \phantom{aaa} C = 40H + 10LH + 40L

Notice in the cost equation that the base dimension is only accounted for once because our container has an open top. Now we isolate for L in the constraint equation and substitute it into the cost equation.

9 = LH \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} L = \frac{9}{H} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} C = 40H + 10\left(\frac{9}{H}\right)H + 40\left(\frac{9}{H}\right) \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} C = 40H + 90 + \frac{360}{H}

Now we differentiate C with respect to H, set C' to 0 and solve for H.

C' = 40 - \frac{360}{H^{2}} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} 0 = 40 - \frac{360}{H^{2}} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} 0 = 40H^{2} - 360 \\ \phantom{aaa} H = 3

Use this value to obtain L = 3, and we have the dimensions that minimize the cost for the container are L = 3m, W = 4m and H = 3m.

Question 3: A sheet of cardboard 3 m. by 4 m. will be made into a box by cutting equal-sized squares from each corner and folding up the four edges. What will be the dimensions of the box with largest volume ?

Let L and W represent the length and width of the cardboard box, respectively and let H represent the length of the square cut out. Then the dimensions of the box are given by

L = 4 - 2H \\ \phantom{aaa} W = 3 - 2H \\ \phantom{aaa} H

The volume, represented by V, is given by

V = (4-2H)(3-2H)H \\ \phantom{aaa} V = 4H^{3} - 14H^{2} + 12H

Now we differentiate V with respect to H, set V' to 0 and solve for H.

V' = 12H^{2} - 28H + 12 \\ \phantom{aaa} 0 = 3H^{2} - 7H + 3 \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} H = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{(-7)^{2} - 4(3)(3)}}{2(3)} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} H = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{13}}{6}

Which gives solutions of 0.57 and 1.77. 1.77 is too large as it will result in a negative dimension for the width. Using H = 0.57, we also get L = 2.86 and W = 1.86. These are the dimensions which will maximize the volume of the box.

Question 4: There are 50 apple trees in an orchard. Each tree produces 800 apples. For each additional tree planted in the orchard, the output per tree drops by 10 apples. How many trees should be added to the existing orchard in order to maximize the total output of trees ?

Let A represent the total apples produced, and let T represent the number of additional trees planted. Then the equation for the number of apples produced is given by A = (#of trees)(#of apples pre tree):

A = (50 + T)(800 - 10T)

Differentiating A with respect to T, setting A' to 0 and solving for T gives

A' = (1)(800-10T) + (50+T)(-10) \\ \phantom{aaa} A' = 300 - 20T \\ \phantom{aaa} 0 = 300 - 20T \\ \phantom{aaa} T = 15

So in order to maximize the number of apples produced, 15 additional apple trees should be planted.
