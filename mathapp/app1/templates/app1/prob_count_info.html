Probability Defined

In popular culture, probability is described as the "chance" of something occuring. For instance, one might say that there is an extremely high probability that the sun will rise tommorow. Conversely, you might venture that there is virtually no probability of an alien coming to visit you in the next 10 seconds. These outcomes, whether it be the sun rising or an alien visitation, are taken from a pool of events and there probabilites are the quantification of the likelihood of them occuring.

In mathematics, we define probability a bit more rigorously. Probability is a function that takes a sample space as its input, and its output has the following criteria:

  • It must be value between 0 and 1, inclusive
  • The sum of all outputs from all possible inputs must be exactly 1

This may already be obvious to you if we say it in a more familiar way: the probability of an event happening must be between 0 and 100%, inclusive, and the probability of all the events put together must be exactly equal to 100%, which means that something has to happen.

There is a simple formula we can use to calculate the probability of single event occuring:

\text{Probability of event A occuring} = \frac{\text{number of ways A can occur}}{\text{number of ways any event in the sample space can occur}}

Consider the following example: A 6 sided dice is rolled. What is the probability that a number larger than 4 is rolled? Here, the sample space are the outcomes given by {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. The desired event is a number larger than 4, which has the outcomes given by {5, 6}. Thus, the probability of rolling a number larger than 5 is given by:

P(\text{number greater than 5}) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}

Some more complex examples

Often it is the case that we have more than one outcome to worry about when trying to compute probabilities. In these cases we must often resort to our organized counting techniques. Take the following example: You are to draw 3 cards from a standard deck of 52 cards. What is the probability that you select 2 kings and 1 ace? The number of ways to select 2 kings if 4 choose 2, and the number of ways to select 1 ace is 4 choose 1. Since these 3 cards are to be part of the same hand, we get the total number of ways to do this by:

n(\text{3 card hands with 2 kings and 1 ace}) = (4C2)(4C1) = 24

Now we must also figure out the number of ways of having 3 cards in our hands, since this will be the total number of possible outcomes in the sample space. This is given by:

n(\text{3 card hands}) = 52C3 = 22 100

Now we can calculate the probability:

P(\text{3 card hand with 2 kings and 1 ace}) = \frac{24}{22100} = \frac{6}{5525}

Now consider a different example: If you roll a pair of six sided dice, what is the probability of rolling a sum of 3 or a sum of 7? Notice the key word or in this question. This implies that the events are disjoint, so we will have to find the probability of each event seperately and sum their result. This is done as follows:

P(\text{sum of 3 or 7}) = P(\text{sum of 3}) + P(\text{sum of 7}) \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = \frac{2}{36} + \frac{6}{36} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = \frac{8}{36} \\ \\ \phantom{aaa} = \frac{2}{9}
